Geophysical Planet Definition

8 planets. 9 with Pluto.
32+ round worlds.

In 2006, after several days of debate in Prague, the International Astronomical Union voted on what counts as a planet. The new definition required a body to clear its orbital neighborhood — a tidy bit of orbital bookkeeping that, almost as a side effect, demoted Pluto. Astronomy textbooks updated; bumper stickers did not.

Twenty years on, the question "how many planets are there?" still gets you a different answer depending on who you ask. Eight, says the IAU. Nine, says anyone who grew up before 2006. The argument is fundamentally over a label — which makes it both unwinnable and not very interesting.

There is a less-fraught question that doesn't depend on a vote: how many bodies in our Solar System are large enough that their own gravity has pulled them into a sphere? This is the Geophysical Planet Definition — a single physical threshold, no orbital politics required. Hydrostatic equilibrium is the line where a body becomes geologically serious: once a body crosses it, you get differentiated layers, internal heat, and a surface old enough to hold a story.

By that measure, the Solar System contains at least 32 confirmed round worlds, with five strong candidates further out. The eight familiar planets, of course. Pluto. Four other dwarf planets. And nineteen moons substantial enough to be planets in their own right, were they not orbiting something larger. From Jupiter's 318 Earth-masses down to Mimas — a snowball barely big enough to hold its shape. This atlas documents all of them.
