Geophysical Planet Definition

The Solar System has 32+ worlds.
You've only heard of 8.

In 2006, the International Astronomical Union voted to define a "planet" as a body that clears its orbital neighborhood — a bureaucratic criterion based on orbital dynamics, not physics. By this rule, Pluto was demoted. Eight worlds survived. More than two dozen real, geologically complex worlds were written out of the story.

The alternative — the Geophysical Planet Definition — holds that any body large enough for its own gravity to pull it into a rounded shape (hydrostatic equilibrium) is a world in the fullest sense. It has differentiated geology, likely internal heat, and sufficient mass to shape its own surface over time.

By that definition, our Solar System contains at least 32 confirmed round worlds, plus five strong candidates awaiting confirmation in the outer dark. This atlas documents all of them — from Jupiter's 318 Earth masses down to tiny Mimas, a snowball barely large enough to hold its shape.
